How This Model Thinks

This model weighs lexical legitimacy, commercial keyword strength, TLD quality, naming structure, and comparable public sales context. It is designed to distinguish between commercially strong keywords, legitimate dictionary-based terms, rare but authentic words, personal-name domains, clean brandables, and weaker low-signal strings without relying on opaque scoring alone.

What It Is Good For
Investors: Directional wholesale and retail framing with cleaner market context.
Brokers: Faster triage for naming quality, buyer plausibility, and positioning.
Founders: Early signal on naming credibility, extension fit, and perceived strength.
Developers: Better context on lexical legitimacy, product suitability, and naming usability.
Important Note
This is a structured directional estimate, not a certified appraisal and not a guaranteed sale outcome.
Exceptional short assets, exact-match commercial terms, and buyer-specific strategic matches can sell materially above any model output.

The Sohadot Domain Valuation Framework

Every estimate produced by the tool above is generated by the Sohadot Domain Valuation Framework — an open, versioned scoring methodology. Unlike black-box appraisal tools, the complete inputs are published below so that investors, brokers, marketplaces, researchers, and automated systems can verify exactly how each figure is derived. The underlying keyword, lexical, and comparable-sales datasets are refreshed weekly.

Step 1 — Lexical classification

The name portion of the domain (the part before the dot) is matched against curated keyword, dictionary, and name datasets and assigned one of six classes. The class sets the base score on a 0–100 scale:

ClassificationBase scoreDefinition
Commercial keyword88Contains at least one recognized high-demand commercial term (e.g. ai, pay, cloud, loans). Each detected keyword adds +4, capped at +12.
Dictionary word76An exact, dictionary-attested English word with mainstream usage.
Rare dictionary word70An authentic but low-frequency dictionary word; receives an additional +4 authenticity bonus.
Personal name62A recognized given name or surname.
Brandable58A clean invented string of 4–12 letters, pronounceable, with no hyphens, digits, or awkward repetition.
Random / low signal18No lexical or commercial foundation; an additional −8 penalty applies.

Dictionary-attested words additionally carry a curated brandability rating in the public lexical datasets: a word rated strong for branding suitability adds +8 raw points and moderate adds +4. The rating is recorded per word in the dataset itself — never assigned at evaluation time — so the same word always receives the same adjustment regardless of who evaluates it.

Step 2 — Extension (TLD) weighting

The extension adds a fixed weight reflecting global resale strength and liquidity:

ExtensionWeightMarket rationale
.com+24Strongest global resale extension and the default for end-user acquisitions.
.ai+18Strong alignment with modern AI naming demand.
.io+14Established fit for software and developer-oriented branding.
.co+10Recognized startup alternative with meaningful resale history.
.org+8Credible for institutions, nonprofits, and communities.
.dev+8Developer-focused branding with growing adoption.
.net+7Legacy alternative with moderate resale liquidity.
All others+4Limited resale liquidity outside niche or geographic demand.

Step 3 — Length adjustment

Shorter names are scarcer and command structural premiums. The adjustment declines with each character:

Name length234567891011+
Adjustment+30+24+18+12+9+7+5+3+1−3

Step 4 — Composite score and valuation tiers

Base score, extension weight, length adjustment, and keyword bonuses are summed, then normalized against the framework's theoretical maximum of 154 raw points (88 base + 12 keyword bonus + 24 extension + 30 length) to produce a 5–100 composite score. Normalization — rather than simple clamping — keeps the top of the scale reserved for genuinely category-defining assets. The score maps to five published tiers:

TierScore rangeInterpretation
Ultra Premium85–100Category-defining assets: exact-match commercial terms, ultra-short names, top extensions.
Premium70–84Strong commercial or lexical foundation with broad buyer plausibility.
Strong55–69Credible naming quality; value depends more on buyer fit than raw demand.
Standard35–54Usable but unremarkable; realistic outcomes cluster near wholesale levels.
Low Signal5–34Weak lexical and commercial foundation; resale demand is unlikely.

Step 5 — Pricing bands: wholesale vs retail

The composite score is converted to a dollar midpoint (midpoint = max($75, score × 16 + extension weight × 14)), and class-specific multipliers produce two distinct bands. Wholesale is the fast, investor-to-investor price achievable in auctions and marketplace liquidations. Retail is the end-user acquisition price — what a business pays for its brand name:

ClassificationWholesale band (× midpoint)Retail band (× midpoint)
Commercial keyword0.72 – 1.452.8 – 6.4
Dictionary word0.68 – 1.382.4 – 5.8
Rare dictionary word0.62 – 1.322.2 – 5.4
Personal name0.60 – 1.252.0 – 4.6
Brandable0.58 – 1.221.9 – 4.4
Random / low signal0.50 – 1.101.2 – 2.0

Step 6 — Comparable-sales calibration

A reported sale only qualifies as a comparable when it shares a real pricing driver with the subject domain — the same lexical classification, a shared commercial keyword, or the exact name. Extension and length proximity alone are never treated as comparability. Qualifying evidence is then applied in a strict hierarchy of strength:

EvidenceWeightTreatment
The asset's own documented saleAnchorA public prior sale of the exact domain outranks any model output: pricing anchors to the sale price (wholesale 0.35–0.65×, retail 0.9–1.4× the recorded sale).
Same name, other extension50%Blended geometrically with the model midpoint at equal weight.
Related comparables30%The median (never the mean) of qualifying comps, blended geometrically with 70% model weight; bands recomputed at wholesale 0.68–1.34×, retail 2.2–5.4×.

Using the median rather than the mean, and blending geometrically rather than linearly, prevents a single outlier sale from inflating an estimate by orders of magnitude — while the anchor rule ensures the model never contradicts the strongest evidence that exists: what the asset itself actually sold for.

Scope and limitations

The framework produces structured directional estimates, not certified appraisals or guaranteed sale outcomes. Exceptional short assets, exact-match commercial terms, and buyer-specific strategic matches routinely sell materially above any model output. Estimates assume an unencumbered domain with no trademark conflicts.

Domain Valuation FAQ

How does the Sohadot domain valuation model work?
It scores a domain in three layers: lexical classification of the name (commercial keyword, dictionary word, rare dictionary word, personal name, brandable, or low signal), market weighting for the extension and length, and calibration against comparable public sales. The composite 5–100 score is then converted into wholesale and retail price bands using published class multipliers.
What is the difference between wholesale and retail domain value?
Wholesale value is what a domain typically fetches in fast, investor-to-investor channels such as auctions and marketplace liquidations. Retail value is what an end user — typically a business acquiring its brand name — pays. In this framework retail runs roughly 2× to 6.4× the model midpoint, while wholesale runs roughly 0.5× to 1.45×.
Which domain extension is most valuable?
.com carries the highest weight in the framework (+24) because it remains the strongest global resale extension. .ai (+18) and .io (+14) follow, driven by AI and software branding demand, then .co (+10), .org (+8), .dev (+8), and .net (+7). All other extensions receive +4.
How does length affect a domain's value?
Shorter is scarcer and more valuable. Two-character names receive the maximum +30 adjustment, declining steadily with each additional character (3 letters +24, 4 letters +18, 5 letters +12) until names of 11 or more characters receive a −3 penalty.
Are automated domain appraisals accurate?
They are directional, not definitive. This framework publishes its full inputs so every estimate can be audited, but no automated model is a certified appraisal: exceptional short assets, exact-match commercial terms, and buyer-specific strategic matches routinely sell far above any model output.
What makes a domain brandable?
In this framework, a brandable is a clean invented string of 4 to 12 letters with no awkward character repetition, a pronounceable structure, and no hyphens or digits. Brandables carry a base score of 58, and their realized value depends heavily on buyer taste and branding fit.
What are comparable sales and how are they used?
Comparables are publicly reported domain sales that share a real pricing driver with the subject domain: the same lexical classification, a shared commercial keyword, or the exact name. Evidence is applied in a strict hierarchy: a documented prior sale of the exact domain anchors the estimate outright, a sale of the same name on another extension carries 50% weight, and related comparables contribute 30% via the median price blended geometrically — so one outlier sale cannot inflate the estimate.
Is the Sohadot domain valuation tool free?
Yes. The valuation tool and the full published methodology are free to use, with no account or signup required.

Domain Valuation Glossary

Standard terminology used across this framework and the domain aftermarket:

Wholesale value
The price a domain typically achieves in fast, investor-to-investor channels such as expired-domain auctions and marketplace liquidations.
Retail value
The price an end user — typically a business acquiring its brand name — pays for a domain; usually a multiple of wholesale value.
Comparable sale (comp)
A publicly reported domain sale that shares the subject domain's extension, classification, length profile, or keywords, used to calibrate an estimate.
Lexical legitimacy
The degree to which a domain name is an authentic, attested word or recognized name rather than an arbitrary string; a core driver of naming credibility.
Brandable domain
A clean invented name, typically 4–12 letters, pronounceable, with no hyphens or digits, valued for branding potential rather than search demand.
Exact-match domain (EMD)
A domain whose name exactly matches a commercial search term (e.g. loans.com), commanding premium pricing from category buyers.
TLD (top-level domain)
The extension after the final dot, such as .com, .ai, or .io; a major weighting factor in resale value and liquidity.
Valuation tier
A quality band derived from the composite score: Ultra Premium (85–100), Premium (70–84), Strong (55–69), Standard (35–54), and Low Signal (5–34).
Liquidity
How quickly a domain can be converted to cash near its wholesale value; .com and short, keyword-rich names are the most liquid.
Drop (expired domain)
A previously registered domain released back to public availability after its registration lapses, often acquired at wholesale prices.
