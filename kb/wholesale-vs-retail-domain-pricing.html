Wholesale vs Retail Domain Pricing: Why the Same Name Has Two Prices

Published June 12, 2026 · Sohadot Reference Layer

The single most common mistake in the domain aftermarket — made by new investors and first-time buyers alike — is treating a domain as having one "true" price. It does not. Every resale-grade domain has two real prices, often separated by a multiple of three to six, and confusing one for the other leads directly to overpaying, underselling, or misreading the entire market.

The two-price reality

Wholesale value is what a domain fetches in fast, investor-to-investor channels: expired-domain auctions, marketplace liquidations, and bulk portfolio trades. The buyer is a professional who intends to resell, so they price in carrying costs, time risk, and their own required margin. Wholesale is a liquidity price — what the asset converts to cash for this week.

Retail value is what an end user pays: a company acquiring its brand name, a funded startup buying its category term, a professional securing their identity. The end user is not buying inventory — they are buying a permanent piece of brand infrastructure they will use for years. Their alternative is not "a different domain at a discount" but a worse name, and that changes what the asset is worth to them entirely.

Rule of thumb: in the Sohadot Valuation Framework, retail bands run roughly 2× to 6.4× the model midpoint while wholesale runs roughly 0.5× to 1.45× — a published, class-specific spread you can audit on the methodology page.

Why the spread exists

The wholesale–retail gap is not inefficiency. It is payment for three real things:

  • Time risk. A quality .com may wait years for its end-user buyer. The investor who carries it through that wait earns the spread; the seller who needs cash now pays it.
  • Matching cost. The end user who values a name at $40,000 exists, but finding them — or being findable when they search — is the actual work of domain investing.
  • Information asymmetry. End users rarely know wholesale levels; investors rarely know which buyer has a strategic need. Each side prices from what it can see.

How to use each price correctly

SituationWhich price governs
Buying at auction to resellPay wholesale or below — your margin is the spread, and overpaying at acquisition cannot be fixed later.
Selling to an end userAnchor to retail. Quoting wholesale to a brand buyer leaves most of the asset's value on the table.
Needing fast liquidityExpect wholesale. Speed is the thing being purchased from you.
Valuing a portfolio honestlyMark to wholesale, dream at retail. Counting every name at retail is how portfolios get fictional valuations.
Buying your own brand nameYou are the end user: a fair retail price for the right name is usually rational, because the alternative is compounding brand cost on a worse name.

Where the confusion does damage

New sellers see a reported $95,000 retail sale of a name like theirs and refuse a fair $4,000 wholesale offer — then carry the name unsold for a decade. New buyers see auction results at $800 and conclude a broker's $25,000 retail quote is a scam — when both numbers are simultaneously real. Reported sales lists mix both markets without labels, which is why a sale price means little until you know which channel it cleared in.

This is also why automated appraisals confuse people: a tool reporting a single number is silently choosing one side of the spread. The Sohadot valuation tool reports both bands separately, with the multipliers published per lexical class, precisely so the two markets never get conflated.

Key takeaways

  • Every resale-grade domain has two real prices; the 2–6× spread between them is structural, not a market error.
  • Wholesale is a liquidity price; retail is a brand-infrastructure price. The channel determines which applies.
  • Acquire at wholesale, market at retail, and mark inventory honestly at wholesale.
  • Never interpret a reported sale without asking which market it cleared in.
