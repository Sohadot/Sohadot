Keyword Strength and Naming Terrain: Why Search Volume Isn't Domain Value

Published June 12, 2026 · Sohadot Reference Layer

"Weather" is searched millions of times a day. Nobody is paying seven figures for weather-themed startup names. "Fintech" is searched a tiny fraction as often — and names containing it clear real money every month. The difference is the most misunderstood concept in keyword-based domain investing: search interest and naming demand are different markets, and only one of them buys domains.

The four properties of a domain-strong keyword

A keyword earns domain value when businesses want to be named by it, not merely when people search it. That requires four properties at once:

PropertyTestWhy volume alone fails it
Commercial intentDoes money change hands near this word? ("pay", "insurance", "loans")"Weather" has volume but no transaction at the end of the search.
Naming usabilityDoes it sound like a company? Can it prefix or suffix cleanly ("payflow", "aibase")?Many high-volume terms are grammatically unusable as brands.
Buyer formationAre new companies being founded — and funded — in this category right now?Domain buyers are mostly new entities; legacy categories generate few of them.
Extension fitDoes the term have a natural home (.com universally; .ai for intelligence terms)?A strong term in a weak wrapper inherits the wrapper's ceiling.

This is why the Sohadot monthly report selects keywords for domain relevance rather than raw popularity, and why the valuation framework maintains a published, curated commercial keyword list (116 terms) instead of importing a search volume database wholesale.

The terrain map: head term, compound, modifier

Within a strong category, position determines value. Think of each keyword as a territory with three rings:

  • The head term (ai.com, insurance.com, crypto.com) — the category itself. One exists per extension; pricing is set by strategic buyers and runs seven to eight figures, as the public sales record shows. In the published framework these are exact-match commercial keywords, the highest-scoring lexical class.
  • Clean compounds (aitools, payflow, cloudbase) — the keyword plus one functional or brandable element. Liquid, abundant enough to trade regularly, scarce enough to hold value. This is where most professional keyword investing actually happens.
  • Modifier sprawl (bestcheapaitools…) — three or more elements, descriptive rather than nameable. Search-engine logic from a bygone era; near-zero resale demand today.
Economic asymmetry of the rings: head terms appreciate with the category and almost never return to market — they are bought to be kept. Compounds track category momentum with high beta: they boom with funding cycles ("ai" compounds post-2022) and fade with them ("crypto" compounds post-peak). Modifier names track nothing, because nothing demands them.

Timing: keywords are cyclical assets

Naming demand follows the funding cycle, not the search graph. A category's keyword strength peaks when new company formation peaks — which is visible quarters before it shows in sales reports. This is the analytical purpose of tracking monthly keyword movement alongside the weekly drops watchlist: the drops market is where cyclical mispricing appears first, as names registered in one cycle expire into the next. When reading any keyword sale as evidence, date-check it against its cycle — a peak-cycle comp is a ceiling marker, not a baseline, as covered in How to Read Comparable Sales.

A note on incidental substrings

A keyword inside a string is not a keyword domain. "Rain" contains "ai"; "scash" contains "cash"; neither carries the keyword's demand, because no buyer reads them that way. Credible models classify by lexical precedence — a real word ranks as a word, and only genuine compounds ("aitools", "payify") inherit keyword status. The published classification rules formalize exactly this, and it is worth checking any appraisal tool you use against the "rain" test.

Key takeaways

  • Search interest and naming demand are different markets; only naming demand buys domains.
  • Domain-strong keywords need all four: commercial intent, naming usability, buyer formation, extension fit.
  • Position in the terrain — head term, compound, modifier — sets the price logic within a category.
  • Keyword value is cyclical with funding, not with search volume; date-check every comp.
  • A keyword inside a string is not a keyword domain — apply the "rain" test.
