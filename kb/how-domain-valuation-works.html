How Domain Valuation Actually Works: Inside a Structured Pricing Model

Published June 12, 2026 · Sohadot Reference Layer

Type a domain into most appraisal tools and you get a single number with no explanation. That number is not a valuation — it is an opinion you cannot audit. Real valuation, in domains as in real estate, is an architecture: separate layers of evidence, each answering a different question, combined by rules you can read. This article walks through that architecture layer by layer.

Why flat scoring fails

Early appraisal tools scored domains on surface features — length, extension, hyphens — and mapped the score to dollars with a fixed curve. The failure mode is structural: surface features cannot distinguish river.com (a dictionary word with universal recognition) from rivex.com (an invented string of identical length and extension), yet the market prices them an order of magnitude apart. Worse, a single opaque number hides which market it describes: as covered in Wholesale vs Retail Domain Pricing, every resale-grade name has two real prices separated by a 2–6× spread. A tool that reports one figure is silently choosing a side without telling you.

Layer 1 — Lexical identity: what is this string?

The first question is not "how much" but "what kind." A name's lexical class determines who could ever want it, and the answer changes the price logic entirely:

Lexical classDemand driverBuyer pool
Exact-match commercial keywordThe name is the category ("ai", "insurance", "loans")Every business in the category — the widest pool that exists
Dictionary wordUniversal recognition and trustBroad and cross-industry, but each buyer needs brand fit
Rare dictionary wordAuthenticity plus distinctivenessNarrow but real: boutique, heritage, and story-driven brands
Personal nameIdentity utilityPeople and family businesses carrying that name
Brandable (invented)Sound, structure, and tasteStartups seeking ownable names — demand is taste-dependent
Random / low signalNoneEffectively no resale market

Classification is a data problem, not a judgment call. The Sohadot framework resolves it against published wordlists — 9,000+ common English words, an extended attested lexicon of 319,000+ entries, 25,000+ French, Spanish, German, Italian and Portuguese words, plus curated rare and commercial term sets — under a strict precedence so that a real word is never mistaken for an invented string, and an incidental substring ("rain" containing "ai") never triggers a false commercial classification.

Layer 2 — Market weighting: scarcity and channel strength

The same string is worth different amounts in different wrappers. Two market facts get weighted on top of the lexical base: extension (.com carries the deepest buyer pool and resale liquidity, with .ai and .io strong in their niches) and length (each character of brevity is a scarcity premium — there are only 676 two-letter strings but 308 million six-letter ones). In the published framework these are explicit numbers — .com +24 down to +4 for fringe extensions, +30 for two characters tapering to −3 at eleven plus — summed and normalized to a 5–100 composite score that maps to five published tiers.

Layer 3 — Evidence: what has the market actually paid?

Formula output is hypothesis; transactions are evidence. The final layer calibrates the model against comparable sales under an evidence hierarchy: a documented prior sale of the exact domain anchors the estimate outright; a same-name sale on another extension carries half the weight; related sales (same class or shared keyword) contribute through their median, blended geometrically so one famous outlier cannot inflate a thousand ordinary estimates. The comps dataset itself is public — every anchor can be inspected.

The economic logic of the hierarchy: evidence weight should match evidence quality. What the asset itself sold for beats what similar assets sold for, which beats what a formula predicts. Most appraisal tools invert this — trusting their curve over the market's receipts.

Why transparency is the accuracy mechanism

Here is the part most tools get backwards: in valuation, transparency is not a marketing feature — it is the error-correction mechanism. A black-box model that misprices a class of names will keep mispricing them, because nobody outside can see why. A published model gets audited by its users: when this framework once let an outlier comp inflate a rare-word estimate by 100×, and later when it underpriced an asset against its own documented $11M sale, both failures were visible, diagnosable, and fixed in the model for everyone — each fix logged in the public changelog. That loop — publish, get challenged, correct, version — is what makes a model converge on the market instead of drifting from it.

What no model can capture

Honest valuation states its limits. No automated model sees buyer-specific strategic value (the startup that just raised funding under that exact name), trademark encumbrances, or the patience of the seller — which is why structured output should be read as directional bands, not gospel, and why exceptional assets routinely clear above any model's ceiling. A model's job is to anchor judgment, not replace it.

Key takeaways

  • Valuation is a three-layer architecture: lexical identity → market weighting → transaction evidence.
  • Classification determines the buyer pool, and the buyer pool determines the price logic.
  • Evidence outranks formula: the asset's own sale history anchors everything.
  • Transparency is the accuracy mechanism — black boxes cannot be error-corrected.
  • Models produce directional bands; strategic premiums belong to judgment.
