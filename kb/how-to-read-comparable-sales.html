How to Read Comparable Domain Sales Correctly

Published June 12, 2026 · Sohadot Reference Layer

Public sales reports are the closest thing the domain market has to ground truth — actual prices that actual buyers paid. They are also the most misused evidence in the industry: cherry-picked to justify fantasy asks, averaged naively into meaningless numbers, and quoted without the one question that gives a sale meaning. This guide covers how professionals actually read comps.

What makes a sale a true comparable

A comparable is not "a domain that sold." It is a sale that shares a real pricing driver with the name you are valuing. Extension and similar length alone do not qualify — by that logic a $3,000,000 sale of a category-defining finance term would be evidence for any seven-letter .com, which is absurd. A sale earns comparable status through:

  • Same lexical class. Dictionary words price against dictionary words, brandables against brandables, exact-match commercial terms against their own kind.
  • Shared commercial keyword. An "ai" name sale is evidence for other "ai" names because the demand driver is identical.
  • The exact name itself. A documented prior sale of the very domain you are valuing is near-definitive evidence — it outranks any model or opinion.
This is published policy at Sohadot: the valuation framework applies comps in a strict evidence hierarchy — the asset's own documented sale anchors the estimate outright, a same-name sale on another extension carries 50% weight, and related comps contribute 30% via the median, blended geometrically so no single outlier can distort the result.

The outlier trap

Reported sales are not a normal distribution — they are a long tail. A handful of famous transactions (insurance.com at $35.6M, voice.com at $30M) sit orders of magnitude above the thousands of unreported $500 wholesale flips. This has two practical consequences:

  1. Never average comps. One landmark sale in a set of four drags a naive mean into fiction. Use the median, which a single outlier cannot move.
  2. Survivorship bias is built in. Sales reports show the names that sold, never the millions that didn't. A category's reported prices say nothing about its sell-through rate, which is usually in the low single digits per year.

The questions that give a comp meaning

QuestionWhy it changes everything
Which channel?Auction (wholesale) and brokered end-user (retail) prices differ by 2–6× for the same quality of name. A sale price without its channel is half a fact. See wholesale vs retail pricing.
When?Category demand moves. AI-name comps from 2023 onward sit far above the same strings' pre-2022 values; crypto comps did the reverse after 2022.
Who bought, and why?A strategic buyer with a product launch pays differently than an investor. Buyer-specific premiums do not transfer to your name.
Is it verified?Marketplace-reported and escrow-verified sales are evidence. Rumored numbers and seller claims are marketing.

What comps can and cannot tell you

Comps validate context, not entitlement. A $95,000 sale of a one-word dictionary .com proves that market exists — it does not grant $95,000 to every adjacent word. Your name still has to pass the same filters that buyer applied: usability, memorability, category fit, and timing. The honest use of a comp is as a ceiling-and-floor frame: it shows what the best-case buyer has actually paid in your class, and what the wholesale channel has actually cleared at.

Key takeaways

  • A comp must share a pricing driver: lexical class, commercial keyword, or the exact name. Extension + length is not comparability.
  • Use medians, never means — domain sales are a long-tail distribution where one outlier wrecks an average.
  • A sale price means little without its channel, date, and buyer context.
  • Comps prove a market exists; they do not entitle adjacent names to the same price.
